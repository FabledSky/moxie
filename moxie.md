# Cafe Moxie Master Brand Template

> Definitive implementation guide for **Cafe Moxie**, a worker-first software shop inside Fabled Sky.  
> This document is the single source of truth for brand, copy, color, visual direction, product presentation, interaction design, and storefront language.  
> Use this file as direct instruction. Do not reinterpret the brand. Do not soften it into generic SaaS language. Do not substitute alternate options.

---

## 1. Brand Definition

**Brand Name:** Cafe Moxie

**Parent Brand:** Fabled Sky

**Brand Type:** Worker-first software shop and digital utility counter

**Core Thesis:** AI is happening. Workers should own the leverage.

**What Cafe Moxie Is:**  
Cafe Moxie is Fabled Sky's worker-first software shop for small desktop tools, local-first utilities, and compute-backed task workflows that help people handle real computer work with more speed, more control, and more dignity.

**What Cafe Moxie Is Not:**  
Cafe Moxie is not enterprise AI transformation software.  
Cafe Moxie is not a workforce replacement platform.  
Cafe Moxie is not a founder-productivity fantasy brand.  
Cafe Moxie is not a generic AI solutions marketplace.  
Cafe Moxie is not a dashboard pretending to be a tool.  
Cafe Moxie is not a subscription treadmill.

**Commercial Model:**  
- Buy once for local tools
- Pay per task for compute-backed workflows
- Keep ownership and control with the worker whenever possible

**Brand Metaphor:**  
Cafe Moxie is a **counter, not a corporation**.  
It should feel like a lit-up software counter on a quiet industrial street at night: a place where capable people walk in, get the right tool, and get back to work sharper than before.

---

## 2. Final Strategic Positioning

**Primary Positioning Line:**  
Tools for people who actually do the work.

**Brand Promise:**  
Cafe Moxie helps workers keep their judgment at the center while using software and AI as practical leverage, not as a replacement for skill, but as a recognition of it.

**Brand Mission:**  
Give workers sharper tools than the systems trying to flatten them.

**Brand Worldview:**  
Workers already know how to do their jobs.  
What they need is not more management software, more workflow abstraction, or more platform dependency.  
What they need is useful leverage: small, sharp tools that respect how real work actually happens.

**Brand Enemy:**  
The experience of being flattened into a workflow by bad software.

**Direct Contrast Statement:**  
Enterprise AI tries to standardize labor. Cafe Moxie equips labor.

**Practical Contrast Statement:**  
Other software asks how to automate this person's job. Cafe Moxie asks how to make this person's Tuesday less annoying.

## 3. Audience Definition

Cafe Moxie is built for people whose jobs happen on computers and whose skill has been shaped by doing the work repeatedly in the real world.

### Primary Audience
- Operations staff
- Coordinators
- Executive assistants
- Administrative assistants
- Analysts
- Researchers
- Editors
- Producers
- Office managers
- Project support staff
- Case managers
- Digital freelancers
- High-competence non-engineer power users

### Audience Traits
- Comfortable with desktop and web workflows
- Not necessarily technical experts, but highly competent
- Deals with repetitive digital tasks constantly
- Has built shortcuts, instincts, habits, and workarounds over years of actual work
- Knows what the output should look like before the tool even runs
- Skeptical of hype and immediately responsive to things that actually work

## 9. Final Visual Direction

### Visual Identity Summary
Cafe Moxie must look like a **neon industrial utility counter** with **midnight dockside diner** energy.

It should feel like:
- A lit-up sign in the dark
- A useful place in an otherwise quiet industrial zone
- A software counter that real operators know about
- A worker refuge with sharp tools
- A practical retro-future environment
- Borderlands-adjacent in energy, but cleaner, smarter, and more trustworthy

### Visual Concept Statement
Cafe Moxie should feel like a neon-lit software counter on a quiet industrial street at night: part diner sign, part repair shop, part digital toolkit wall. Dark surroundings, electric signals, warm human light. A place for workers who know what they are doing and just want sharper tools.

### Visual Rules
- Dark base
- Neon signal accents
- Warm human light
- Quiet space around lit elements
- Industrial structure without grime overload
- Slightly rugged shapes, but clear and legible
- Interface first, decoration second
- Glow used as signal, not noise

### Do Not Make It Look Like
- A sterile SaaS company
- Generic cyberpunk
- A tech conference brand
- A luxury design system
- A maximalist gamer site
- A full steampunk cosplay brand
- A comic-book parody of Borderlands

### Borderlands Influence Rules
Borrow:
- Rugged modularity
- Hazard-strip micro accents
- Salvaged tool energy
- Loud signage
- Industrial confidence
- Slight irreverence

Do not borrow:
- Constant yellow everywhere
- Visual clutter
- Aggressive macho tone
- Over-distressed textures
- Comic-book chaos
- Jagged unreadable UI panels

## 15. Product Architecture

Cafe Moxie products must be classified exactly like this.

### A. Local Tool
Definition: A standalone desktop application or packaged local utility.

Attributes:
- One-time purchase
- Repeat use
- Runs on the user's machine
- User-owned capability

Storefront Label:
**Buy Once**

### B. Compute Task
Definition: A cloud-backed task, pipeline, or transformation run using compute credits.

Attributes:
- Task-based usage
- Paid per run or per unit
- Best for heavier or specialist work
- Convenience over local setup

Storefront Label:
**Pay Per Task**

### C. Hybrid Tool
Definition: A local tool with optional compute-backed extension.

Attributes:
- User owns baseline capability
- Compute activates only when needed

Storefront Label:
**Buy Once + Compute When Needed**

### Commerce Rule
Always communicate the buying model in plain language before the technical model.

## 16. Compute Credits Rules

### Customer-Facing Term
Use **Compute Credits**.

### Internal/Technical Term
If needed internally, Fabled Units of Compute may exist in backend systems.  
Do not lead with that term in customer-facing storefront copy.

### Customer Explanation
Compute Credits are pay-as-needed credits for tasks that run on Cafe Moxie infrastructure instead of the user's machine.

### Required Compute Line
Use compute when a job is too heavy, too specialized, or simply not worth setting up locally.

### Compute Commerce Principle
The user is not paying for vague platform access.  
The user is paying for the work required to run the task.

### Approved Compute Copy
- Pay for the task, not the platform.
- Use compute when local setup is not worth it.
- Heavier lift. Same judgment.
- Run it off-site when you just need it done.

## 20. Product Page Template

Every product page must answer these questions in this order.

### 1. What's the annoying task?
Lead with the pain in plain language.

### 2. Who deals with this?
List the actual roles that encounter the problem.

### 3. What goes in?
Describe the inputs clearly.

### 4. What comes out?
Describe the outputs clearly.

### 5. Local or compute?
State the execution mode clearly.

### 6. How is it priced?
Show the actual price. Never hide pricing behind contact sales.

### 7. What still needs your judgment?
State what needs human review or spot-checking.

### 8. Why this saves you time
Describe the concrete time or friction reduction.

### 9. Who else uses this?
Add a short social-proof context line.

### Mandatory Product Page Principle
Always respect the user enough to be honest about where the tool ends and the operator begins.

## 21. Example Product Copy

### BatchScan Clean

**Tagline:**  
Turn stacks of scanned PDFs into searchable, copyable documents.

**What's the annoying task?**  
You have a pile of scanned PDFs that look fine until you try to search, copy, or reuse anything inside them. Then they are just pictures of text trapped in a file.

**Who deals with this?**  
Legal assistants, records managers, researchers, operations coordinators, and administrative staff.

**What goes in?**  
Scanned PDFs. Single files or full folders.

**What comes out?**  
Searchable, copyable PDFs with preserved layout and an OCR text layer.

**Local or compute?**  
Runs Local.

**Price:**  
$24 one-time purchase.

**What still needs your judgment?**  
Spot-check tables, headers, and handwritten annotations. Clean type converts well. Messier layouts may need a second look.

**Why this saves you time:**  
Without this, you are opening files one by one and manually extracting text. With this, you drop in a folder and the batch runs in minutes.

**Who else uses this?**  
Administrative teams, paralegals, and academic researchers processing high-volume document requests.
